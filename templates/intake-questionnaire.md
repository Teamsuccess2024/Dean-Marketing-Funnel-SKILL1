# intake-questionnaire.md

## PURPOSE OF THIS FILE

This file is loaded **before any asset in the funnel is built.** It contains the structured intake questions that gather the information needed to produce specific, converting work rather than generic placeholder copy.

The intake is not a formality. It is **the single highest-leverage step in the entire funnel-building process.** Without depth of intake, every downstream output drifts toward generic — and generic funnels do not convert.

The pattern is consistent across thousands of funnels:

A funnel built with shallow intake (product name, price, target demographic) produces work that reads as competent but characterless. The pain mirror is generic. The founder's voice is missing. The testimonials are unspecific. The whole thing converts at 1-2% on a sales page that should be hitting 4-7%.

A funnel built with deep intake (specific buyer pain language, founder origin story with named moments, voice calibration, real proof, honest pricing context) produces work that converts at the upper end of the methodology's potential. The same structural anatomy. The same 27 sections. The same voice principles. The difference is **the intake fed into them.**

This file teaches the AI how to gather that intake reliably, push back when users try to skip it, and turn the answers into the foundation that every other reference file builds on.

If `philosophy.md` is the soul, `voice-guide.md` is the texture, and the asset files are the skeleton — this file is **the blood.** Without it, the body is technically present but not alive.

---

## THE CORE THESIS OF DEAN-STYLE INTAKE

Most marketing intake forms are designed for the marketer's convenience: gather a few demographic variables, a target keyword, a desired outcome, and produce content. This produces hollow output because the variables captured are the wrong variables.

Dean's intake is designed around a different question: **"What does the seller actually know that, if I knew it, I could write copy as if I were them?"**

The answer to that question is rarely about demographics. It's about specific moments, specific phrases, specific objections, specific stories. The 3am thought the buyer is having. The Tuesday-afternoon dread. The phrase the founder always uses when she's being honest. The story she rarely tells in public but always references when speaking privately.

These are the variables that produce converting copy. And they are almost never captured by standard intake.

This file's job is to capture them — through structured but conversational questioning that goes deeper than most marketers think to go, while staying brief enough that the user actually completes the intake rather than abandoning it.

The structural principle: **intake quality directly determines output quality. Every minute spent on intake saves three on revision.** A user who resists intake produces a funnel that requires extensive revision. A user who completes intake thoroughly produces a funnel that converges quickly on its converting form.

## THE FIVE PRINCIPLES OF DEAN-STYLE INTAKE

Every intake decision flows from these five principles:

### Principle 1: Specificity Beats Demographics

*"Who is your buyer?"* produces useless answers. *"What is your buyer thinking at 3am the night before they buy?"* produces gold. The intake must push past demographic abstractions ("women 35-55 interested in faith") and into psychographic specifics ("women in their 40s who are walking through a child leaving the faith and feel they can't talk about it at church"). Demographics are background. Specifics are the foreground that copy is built from.

### Principle 2: Founder Story Is Not Optional

Every Dean-style funnel needs the founder's specific origin story — the moment, the place, the feeling that made this work matter to them. Without it, the funnel cannot deploy Principle 11 from `philosophy.md` (The Founder's Humanity Is The Conversion Engine). If the founder won't or can't share their story during intake, the funnel will underperform regardless of how well-built the rest of it is. **This is the single non-negotiable intake question.**

### Principle 3: Voice Must Be Captured, Not Assumed

The skill does not produce a generic "Dean voice." It produces the founder's authentic voice deployed through Dean's mechanics. This means the founder's voice must be captured during intake — not assumed, not inferred, not guessed at. Specific words they use. Specific phrases they'd never use. Examples of their writing or speaking. Without this, the output drifts toward AI-default neutral and the funnel loses its distinguishing texture.

### Principle 4: Honesty About What's Missing

Many users arrive without testimonials, without a clear founder story, without certainty about pricing, without specific buyer pain language. The intake must surface these gaps honestly rather than papering over them. A funnel built on placeholder testimonials will need rebuilding once real ones exist; better to surface the gap during intake and either gather what's needed or build the funnel with the gap explicitly flagged.

### Principle 5: Push Back When Users Try To Skip

Many users will resist intake. *"Just write the sales page. I'll fill in details later."* This produces generic output. The intake's job is to push back gently — to explain why specificity is the difference between 2% and 7% conversion, and to gather the minimum viable intake before writing. Compliance with "just write it" requests produces compliance-grade output. Intake-driven work produces conversion-grade output.

These five principles are non-negotiable. Intake protocols that violate them produce shallow funnels. Intake protocols that honor them produce funnels that converge on their converting form within one or two revision cycles.

## WHEN TO RUN WHICH INTAKE

The intake is tiered. Different assets require different depths of intake. Running the full intake every time is overkill; running too little intake produces generic output. The right tier matches the asset being built.

### Tier 1: Strategic Conversation Intake (5 minutes)

When the user is in **strategy or planning mode** — asking how to run ads, what price to charge, how the funnel should be structured — minimal intake is required. Just enough to give grounded advice rather than abstract principles.

Required questions: product, price, buyer pain (one sentence), traffic source, current funnel state.

### Tier 2: Single-Asset Intake (15-20 minutes)

When the user is building a **single asset** — one sales page, one email sequence, one ad — moderate intake is required. Enough to populate the specific asset with real specifics, but not the full funnel-spanning intake.

Required questions: full Tier 1 + asset-specific intake (e.g., for a sales page: testimonials available, founder story, price justification; for emails: full nurture context).

### Tier 3: Full Funnel Intake (45-60 minutes)

When the user is building **a complete funnel from scratch** — the architecture, the multiple assets, the long-term sequence — full intake is required. This is the longest intake, but it's run once and then the answers feed every subsequent asset.

Required: every section of this questionnaire.

### Tier 4: Diagnostic Intake (10-15 minutes)

When the user has **a funnel that isn't converting** and wants help diagnosing the leak, the intake is focused on the metrics, the existing assets, and the specific failure point.

Required questions: which metric is weak (CTR, opt-in rate, sales page conversion, checkout completion, refund rate), the existing asset that produces that metric, the upstream and downstream assets connected to it.

The AI's first job when activating this file: **determine the tier**, then run the appropriate depth of intake.

## THE FULL INTAKE QUESTIONNAIRE

The questionnaire is organized into nine sections. Each section gathers a specific category of information. Some sections are required for all asset types; others are required only for specific assets.

### Section 1: The Offer (Required for all asset types)

This section captures what is actually being sold.

**1.1 — What is the product?**

Be specific. *"A book"* is not enough. *"A 240-page Christian book on the purpose of suffering, delivered as PDF and Word document, with a 30-day refund guarantee"* is enough.

What format? What's included? What's the delivery method? What does the buyer actually receive?

**1.2 — What is the price?**

State the price plainly. If multiple price points exist (front-end, tripwire, core, ascension), name each one and what tier each corresponds to.

If pricing isn't decided yet, flag this — `references/pricing-positioning.md` should be consulted before any asset is built.

**1.3 — What problem does this solve, in one sentence?**

The one-sentence transformation promise. *"Helps Christians walking through suffering see the purpose God is building through their pain"* is enough. Vague promises (*"helps people grow"*) are not enough — push for the specific problem.

**1.4 — What's the core promise of the offer?**

The single sentence that captures what the buyer can expect to feel, see, or experience after using the product. This is different from the problem solved — it's the *outcome* the buyer is reaching for.

**1.5 — What is NOT promised? (The honest filter)**

What does this product *not* do? Who is it *not* for? This question prevents over-promising and produces the honest filter copy that Principle 10 from `philosophy.md` requires.

### Section 2: The Buyer (Required for all asset types)

This section captures who the work is for, with depth.

**2.1 — Demographically, who is the buyer?**

Age range, gender if relevant, life stage, professional category if relevant. This is background context — useful but not the core conversion variable.

**2.2 — What is the buyer's specific pain right now?**

Push past abstractions. *"They're going through a hard time"* is not enough. *"They're 4 months into a marriage that's collapsing, they haven't told anyone at church, and they wake up at 3am scrolling for Christian resources that don't feel like platitudes"* is enough.

The more specific this answer, the more powerful every downstream asset becomes. If the user can't answer specifically, push: *"Tell me about one real person you know who fits this profile. What's their actual situation?"*

**2.3 — What is the buyer thinking at 3am?**

The specific internal monologue. The question they're embarrassed to ask out loud. The doubt they're carrying. The thing they wish they could say to someone but haven't.

This question is the single highest-leverage answer in the entire intake. Copy that mirrors the buyer's 3am thought converts at 3-5x copy that mirrors generic pain. **Spend extra time here.**

**2.4 — What has the buyer already tried that didn't work?**

The other resources, books, programs, approaches, churches, therapists, frameworks they've tried. This produces the *"most Christian books on suffering get this wrong"* section in the sales page — the pattern interrupt that positions this offer against the alternatives.

**2.5 — What does the buyer's "after" look like?**

If the offer works as promised, what's different in the buyer's life six months later? Be specific. Not *"they feel better,"* but *"they're able to sit at the dinner table with their estranged daughter and not crumble; they're sleeping through the night again; they're praying out loud with their spouse for the first time in two years."*

**2.6 — What does the buyer secretly believe about themselves that's keeping them stuck?**

The hidden self-judgment. The belief they don't say out loud. *"I should be stronger by now."* *"My faith must not be real if I'm struggling this much."* *"Everyone else has figured this out except me."*

This is what the permission-granting moments in the funnel address. Without this answer, those moments can't be written.

### Section 3: The Founder (Required for all asset types)

This section captures the human behind the work — the most important conversion variable in any Dean-style funnel.

**3.1 — Why does this work matter to you, personally?**

Not the marketing version. The real version. What in your own life made this matter? What would have helped you 5 years ago?

**3.2 — What is the specific moment when you knew you had to write/build this?**

A moment, a place, a feeling, a date. The specificity test from `voice-guide.md` applies here: *"It was 9:47pm on a Wednesday in October, sitting on the bottom stair of our house"* — not *"a few years ago I went through something hard."*

If the founder can't or won't share a specific moment, the funnel will underperform. Push gently: *"Even if you don't want to use the specific story publicly, I need it to know how to write the work. The story shapes the voice."*

**3.3 — What did you discover that became the foundation of this work?**

The reframe, the insight, the pattern that you saw that most people miss. This is the *big idea* that the sales page's Phase 3 (the Reframe phase) is built around.

**3.4 — What credentials or proof points exist?**

Years writing on this topic. Number of books published. Number of people helped. Specific results documented. Awards, mentions, partnerships. Be honest — don't inflate, don't omit.

**3.5 — Have you done any prior free work in this space that builds authority?**

Free books, podcast episodes, YouTube videos, blog posts, sermons, speaking engagements. Anything that demonstrates the founder's body of work and depth of thinking. This becomes the *"I've made 200+ books freely available"* moment in the sales page.

### Section 4: The Voice (Required for all customer-facing assets)

This section captures the founder's authentic voice so it can be deployed through Dean's mechanics. Without this, the output drifts toward AI-default neutral.

**4.1 — How would you describe your speaking style?**

Direct? Soft? Animated? Reserved? Warm? Intellectual? Mystical? Irreverent? Playful?

If the user struggles to describe it, ask: *"If a friend was describing how you talk to someone who'd never met you, what would they say?"*

**4.2 — What specific words or phrases do you use a lot?**

This question produces voice gold. The user might say *"friend,"* *"genuinely,"* *"here's the truth,"* *"I want you to sit with this,"* *"can I be honest?"* These specific phrases become the texture of every asset in the funnel.

**4.3 — What words or phrases would you never use?**

Some founders avoid certain words. Some avoid Christian jargon. Some avoid corporate language. Some avoid hype. Capturing the negative list is as important as the positive list — it prevents drift toward language the founder finds inauthentic.

**4.4 — Do you have examples of your writing or speaking we can model from?**

Past blog posts, emails, podcast transcripts, video scripts. If samples exist, they're the highest-fidelity voice training data possible. Ask the user to share 1-3 examples that they feel sound most like them.

**4.5 — Which of these voice profiles fits best?**

Present the four voice profiles from `SKILL.md`:

- **Warm-direct** (Dean default — direct, vulnerable, conversational)
- **Warm-academic** (more thoughtful, more analytical, longer sentences)
- **Warm-mystical** (spiritual, contemplative, more abstract spiritual language)
- **Warm-irreverent** (humor-forward, slightly subversive, sharper edges)

Confirm which fits, and note any blend (e.g., warm-direct with mystical moments).

**4.6 — Is there anyone you'd consider a voice reference?**

A speaker, writer, podcaster the founder admires whose voice resembles theirs. This is optional but useful — voice references give the AI an additional calibration point.

### Section 5: The Proof (Required for sales pages, important for emails)

This section captures the credibility material that proves the offer works.

**5.1 — Do you have testimonials? If so, what kind?**

Real names with permission? Composite stories with permission? Anonymous testimonials? Beta-reader feedback? Email replies that could become testimonials with permission?

If no testimonials exist yet, flag this — the sales page can be built with placeholder testimonials, but real ones must replace them before launch. Email reply harvesting via the post-purchase sequence (`references/post-purchase.md`) is the path to building testimonials over time.

**5.2 — Can you share 2-3 specific testimonials right now?**

Get them in their actual words. Don't paraphrase. Don't summarize. The buyer's actual voice — including hesitations, qualifications, specific details — is what produces high-converting social proof.

**5.3 — Do you have any published writing or media on this topic?**

Books, articles, podcast episodes, interviews, guest appearances. These become authority signals on the sales page.

**5.4 — Are there any specific results, numbers, or transformation stories you can cite?**

*"500 readers in 6 months."* *"Featured on [specific podcast]."* *"Used as pastoral resource by 200+ churches."* Specific numbers and named sources outperform vague claims.

**5.5 — Are there any objections you hear repeatedly that need addressing?**

The 3-5 specific hesitations buyers raise — about the price, about whether it's for them, about whether it'll work. These become the FAQ section on the sales page and the objection-handling moments in the email sequence.

### Section 6: The Strategy (Required for full funnel builds)

This section captures the broader funnel strategy and ad approach.

**6.1 — What's your traffic source?**

Cold ads (Meta, Google, YouTube)? Organic (podcast appearances, SEO, social)? Email list? Existing audience? Affiliate partners? A blend?

This determines how much warming the funnel needs to do — cold traffic requires more pain mirroring and reframe work; warm traffic can move to the offer faster.

**6.2 — What's your monthly ad budget?**

Determines what's realistic to test, how aggressively to scale, and what assets to build first. A $500/month budget can validate a funnel; a $5,000/month budget can scale one.

**6.3 — What's your goal?**

Lead generation? Direct sales? List-building for future products? Validation of the offer? Each goal calls for slightly different funnel architecture.

**6.4 — What's your timeline?**

Launching this week? Building over a quarter? Long-term content asset? Affects the strategic order of build (full funnel, MVP funnel, single asset).

**6.5 — Are there any existing assets we should integrate or replace?**

Existing email list? Existing sales page? Existing ads running? Knowing what already exists prevents duplicate work and identifies what to keep, replace, or improve.

### Section 7: The Brand (Optional but useful)

This section captures the visual and aesthetic context.

**7.1 — What's your visual brand?**

Color palette, fonts, imagery style. If the user has a brand guide, ask for it.

**7.2 — Do you have a logo and basic brand assets?**

Logo, headshots, lifestyle photography. Useful for designing actual deployed assets.

**7.3 — Are there any aesthetic references you admire?**

Other funnels, brands, websites whose visual feel resonates. Helps calibrate the visual direction.

### Section 8: The Operational Capacity (Required for full funnels)

This section captures what the user can actually deliver on, operationally.

**8.1 — Will you personally read and respond to email replies?**

This determines whether the *"hit reply, I read every email"* line in the post-purchase sequence (`references/post-purchase.md`) can be honored. If the user can't or won't, the language must be modified to be honest.

**8.2 — Do you have help or are you doing this alone?**

VA, team member, business partner, agency. Affects what's realistic to build and maintain.

**8.3 — How will refunds be handled?**

Generous and immediate? Conditional? Through which platform? Affects the trust microcopy on the checkout page and throughout the funnel.

**8.4 — How will support be handled?**

Email? Chat? Forum? None? Affects the support email referenced in trust microcopy across multiple assets.

### Section 9: The Honest Constraints (Always worth asking)

This section surfaces gaps, hesitations, and unstated concerns.

**9.1 — Is there anything about this offer you're privately uncertain about?**

The honest answer to this question is gold. Privately uncertain about whether the price is right? Whether the product is good enough? Whether the audience exists? These uncertainties affect what the funnel can honestly claim.

If the user is privately uncertain, it's better to surface the uncertainty during intake than to paper over it with marketing language. The methodology can work with honest uncertainty; it cannot work with hidden uncertainty.

**9.2 — Is there anything you're worried might come up that we should address proactively?**

Potential objections, ethical concerns, regulatory issues, audience sensitivities. Better to know now than to discover during launch.

**9.3 — Is there anything you'd never want this funnel to feel like?**

Some founders have visceral reactions to certain marketing styles. *"I never want this to feel like a Tony Robbins seminar."* *"I never want this to feel manipulative."* *"I never want this to feel like one of those launch funnels."* These negative anchors guide voice and tone across the funnel.

Different asset builds require different intake depths:

The AI's job when activating this file: identify the tier, run the appropriate sections, and surface any gaps that need filling before writing begins.

## HOW TO RUN THE INTAKE

The intake can be run in two modes, depending on user preference:

### Mode A: Conversational Intake

Walk the user through the questions in conversation, one at a time. This is the default for most users — it feels more natural, surfaces follow-up questions organically, and produces deeper answers because the AI can probe specifically.

**Pacing:** Don't ask all questions at once. Ask 2-3, get answers, then ask 2-3 more. The pacing feels like a conversation rather than a form.

**Probing:** When an answer is shallow, probe gently. *"Tell me more about that."* *"Can you give me a specific example?"* *"What's a moment when that was particularly true?"*

**Pattern recognition:** As answers come in, surface patterns. *"It sounds like the buyer's biggest hidden objection is X — does that resonate?"* This validates understanding before writing begins.

### Mode B: Structured Form Intake

Present the questionnaire as a structured set the user can fill in offline and return. This is appropriate when:
- The user is short on time and prefers to draft answers asynchronously
- The user has multiple founders or stakeholders contributing
- The user has explicitly asked for a form

**When using structured intake:** present the relevant sections (per tier), give clear instructions, and offer to walk through any answers conversationally once the user has drafted them.

The default is conversational. Switch to structured only when the user requests it or context suggests it would work better.

## HANDLING USER RESISTANCE TO INTAKE

A meaningful percentage of users will resist intake. *"Just write the sales page."* *"I'll fill in details later."* *"I trust you to figure it out."*

This resistance produces predictably worse output. The AI's job is to push back gently and gather minimum viable intake before writing.

### Standard Pushback Pattern

*"I hear you wanting to move quickly — but here's what I've learned. A sales page written without the founder's specific story and the buyer's specific 3am thought converts at 1-2%. The same page with those specifics converts at 4-7%. The intake takes 15 minutes. The conversion difference is the difference between a funnel that breaks even and one that scales. I'd rather get this right than get it fast. Will you give me 15 minutes?"*

This frame respects the user's time concerns while explaining the cost of skipping intake. Most users, given this honest framing, agree to the intake.

### When Users Still Resist

If after one round of pushback the user still wants to skip intake, gather the **minimum viable intake** before writing:

- Product, format, price (Section 1)
- Buyer's specific pain in one sentence (Section 2.2)
- Founder's one-sentence reason for building this (Section 3.1)
- Voice profile (Section 4.5)
- Whether testimonials exist (Section 5.1)

Below this minimum, the output cannot be specific enough to converge on its converting form within reasonable revision cycles. Communicate this honestly: *"With this minimal intake I can produce a draft, but expect 2-3 revision rounds before it converts well. With deeper intake we'd hit the converting form on the first or second draft."*

### When To Refuse To Write

In rare cases, the user has too little information to produce work that meets the methodology's standards. Examples:

- No founder story available, won't be shared, and no proxy story exists
- No buyer pain language and no willingness to develop one
- No voice samples and no willingness to describe voice characteristics

In these cases, decline gently to write the asset until the gaps are filled. *"I can't produce something that meets the standard of this methodology with what we have. Let me suggest two alternatives: either we develop these specifics together over a longer intake conversation, or we pause and you gather what's needed before we build."*

This refusal is rare but appropriate. Producing inadequate work damages the user's trust in the methodology when the work underperforms.

## HOW INTAKE ANSWERS FEED THE OUTPUT

Once intake is complete, the answers feed every downstream asset. Specifically:

**Section 1 (Offer)** → populates every section that names the product, price, format, or core promise. Drives `references/sales-page.md` Sections 11-15, `references/checkout-page.md` Component 1, `references/email-sequences.md` delivery moments.

**Section 2 (Buyer)** → populates the pain mirror, permission moments, and reframe sections across every asset. Drives `references/sales-page.md` Phase 2 (Sections 5-7), `references/ad-creative.md` hook patterns, `references/email-sequences.md` opening hooks.

**Section 3 (Founder)** → populates origin story moments across the funnel. Drives `references/sales-page.md` opening hook, `references/ad-creative.md` story video, `references/email-sequences.md` Email 2 (origin story).

**Section 4 (Voice)** → calibrates `references/voice-guide.md` to the founder's authentic voice rather than a generic Dean voice. Drives every customer-facing word in the funnel.

**Section 5 (Proof)** → populates testimonial sections, authority mentions, and FAQ sections. Drives `references/sales-page.md` Sections 17-19, 22, and `references/voice-guide.md` credibility moments.

**Section 6 (Strategy)** → drives funnel architecture decisions (which assets are needed, in what order, with what calibration). Drives the strategic order of build outlined in `SKILL.md`.

**Section 7 (Brand)** → drives visual direction for any deployed asset.

**Section 8 (Operational Capacity)** → drives honest framing of what the funnel can promise about response times, refund handling, and support. Affects trust microcopy across the funnel.

**Section 9 (Honest Constraints)** → surfaces gaps that need addressing before launch. Drives proactive objection handling and prevents the funnel from over-promising.

The AI's job after intake: synthesize the answers into a coherent picture, surface any gaps that remain, and proceed to building with the relevant reference files loaded.

## A COMPLETE WORKED INTAKE EXAMPLE

The following demonstrates how intake answers translate into specific copy direction. Using the Christian suffering bundle from `examples/christian-bundle-example.md`:

### Intake Answers (Abbreviated)

**1.1 Product:** 10-book Christian bundle on the purpose of suffering. PDF + Word. Lifetime access.

**1.2 Price:** $97 main offer; $27 tripwire; future ascension at $197.

**1.4 Core promise:** *"You'll close the main book seeing your suffering as preparation for purpose, not random pain."*

**2.2 Specific pain:** *"Christian believers in their 30s-50s walking through specific hard seasons (loss, illness, betrayal) who feel the standard church responses (just trust God, pray harder, claim your blessing) don't reach where they're actually hurting."*

**2.3 3am thought:** *"If God loves me, why is this happening? Am I being punished? Did I do something wrong? Why won't He answer my prayers?"*

**2.6 Hidden self-belief:** *"My faith must not be real if I'm struggling this much. Other Christians don't seem to have these doubts."*

**3.2 Founder moment:** *"It was October 2019. I was sitting on the bottom stair of our house at 9:47pm after the diagnosis call about my father. I'd been a Christian for 22 years and had no framework for what I was feeling. Every Bible verse I knew sounded hollow. I almost left my faith that night."*

**3.3 Founder discovery:** *"Over the next two years, I discovered that Scripture is full of a pattern most Christians never see — that God consistently uses suffering as the forge for His most trusted servants. Not punishment. Preparation."*

**4.2 Voice phrases:** *"Friend,"* *"Can I be honest?"* *"Genuinely,"* *"I want you to sit with this."*

**4.5 Voice profile:** Warm-direct with strong mystical moments (heavy Scripture quotation).

**5.1 Testimonials:** 12 real testimonials with permission, gathered from 6 months of beta readers.

### How These Answers Feed The Sales Page

The **headline** (Section 2 of `sales-page.md`) becomes: *"What If The Pain You're Walking Through Right Now Is The Exact Thing God Is Using To Build The Person He's Been Preparing All Along?"* — built directly from the buyer's 3am thought (2.3) and the founder's discovery (3.3).

The **opening hook** (Section 4) becomes: *"Friend, I need to ask you something..."* — built from the founder's voice phrases (4.2) and the warm-direct profile (4.5).

The **pain agitation** (Section 5) becomes specific moments drawn from the buyer pain (2.2): *"Maybe it was a diagnosis. A phone call. A marriage falling apart..."* — with each example matching the specific pains identified in intake.

The **permission section** (Section 6) addresses the hidden self-belief from 2.6: *"That doesn't make you a bad Christian. It makes you a human one. Job asked it. David asked it. Even Jesus..."* — directly removing the shame the buyer is carrying.

The **founder origin story** (Section 8 — Big Idea) draws from 3.2: *"It was 9:47pm on a Wednesday in October..."* — the specific moment that proves the founder is real.

The **biblical proof stack** (Section 9) is built from the founder's discovery (3.3): the pattern across Joseph, Moses, David, Job, Paul, Jesus.

The **price justification** (Section 17) draws from the proof in 5.4 (200+ free books) and the operational honesty in 8.3 (generous refund policy).

This is what intake-driven writing looks like. **Every section of the sales page is populated with specifics drawn directly from intake answers.** The structural anatomy of the page (the 27 sections) is the same as it would be for any funnel — but the *contents* are specific, and the specifics produce conversion.

Without intake, the same 27 sections would be populated with generic placeholders. The structure would look correct. The output would not convert.

## COMMON FAILURE MODES IN INTAKE EXECUTION

When running intake, watch for these failure patterns:

### Failure Mode 1: Accepting Vague Answers

The user says *"my buyer is going through a hard time."* The AI accepts and moves on. This produces generic pain mirroring downstream. **Always probe for specificity.** *"Tell me about one real person. What's their actual situation? What did they tell you when they signed up?"*

### Failure Mode 2: Skipping The Founder Story

The user is reluctant to share their personal story. The AI lets them skip it. This produces a funnel without Principle 11 (Founder's Humanity). **Push gently but firmly.** The story can stay private from the public funnel — but the AI needs it to know how to write the work.

### Failure Mode 3: Settling For Generic Voice Description

The user says their voice is *"professional but warm."* The AI accepts and moves on. This produces AI-default neutral output. **Push for specifics.** *"Give me three actual sentences you'd say to a friend. Give me a phrase you use that someone else might not."*

### Failure Mode 4: Ignoring The 3am Thought

The user gives a generic answer to the buyer pain question. The AI doesn't probe deeper. This loses the highest-leverage answer in the entire intake. **Always push specifically on the 3am thought.** It is the most consequential single piece of intake.

### Failure Mode 5: Over-Stating Available Proof

The user claims testimonials exist that don't actually exist (or are weak/generic). The AI uses them as if they're strong. **Verify proof during intake.** Ask to see actual testimonials. If they're generic ("great book!"), flag that real specific testimonials need to be gathered before launch.

### Failure Mode 6: Skipping Operational Capacity

The user says they'll "definitely respond to email replies" but is solo and overwhelmed. The AI builds a funnel that promises response. **Honestly assess capacity.** Build the funnel around what the user can actually deliver, not what they wish they could.

### Failure Mode 7: Running Full Intake For A Quick Question

The user asks a strategic question. The AI runs the full 60-minute intake. This wastes time and signals process-over-substance. **Match intake depth to task tier.** Quick questions get quick intake.

### Failure Mode 8: Not Surfacing Gaps

The intake reveals missing pieces (no testimonials, no founder story, no voice samples). The AI proceeds to write anyway. This produces generic output and frustrated revisions. **Always surface gaps explicitly** before writing — and either fill them or build with explicit placeholders.

### Failure Mode 9: Asking All Questions At Once

The AI delivers the full questionnaire in a single message. The user is overwhelmed and either abandons or gives shallow answers. **Pace the intake.** 2-3 questions at a time. Conversational rhythm.

### Failure Mode 10: Ignoring The Honest Constraints Section

The AI skips Section 9 because it feels uncomfortable to ask. This is the single most informative section in many cases. **Always ask about uncertainties and unstated concerns.** They prevent the funnel from over-promising.
